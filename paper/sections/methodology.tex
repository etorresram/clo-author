\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:method}

The identification strategy is a difference-in-differences (DiD) design with continuous treatment and a single adoption date, estimated \emph{country by country}. Each of the six countries (Costa Rica, Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico, Peru, and Uruguay) is analyzed separately. This is not a pooled cross-country panel: the data are six independent country-level repeated cross-sections, and each is analyzed in its own regression. Occupations within a country are exposed to different intensities of LLM capability, measured by the Eloundou complementary-exposure score $\beta$. ChatGPT's launch on 30 November 2022 is the common treatment date. It is the same calendar date for all six countries --- a single global event, not a staggered policy roll-out --- so country-by-country regressions share the same $t^* = 2022$Q4 (dropped as a buffer; treatment effective from 2023Q1 onward). Identification within each country comes from within-occupation variation in how exposure interacts with the post-launch indicator. The cross-country comparison emerges from the side-by-side display of the six estimated $\hat\tau^c$ values. This section details the sample, the treatment variable, the primary and secondary specifications, the identifying assumptions, the within-country heterogeneity analysis centered on baseline formality, the robustness layer, and the four pre-registered narratives.

The six countries were selected on two criteria. First, occupation coding compatibility. Five (Costa Rica, Colombia, Ecuador, Peru, Uruguay) publish microdata coded directly in ISCO-08 at the four-digit level. Mexico publishes in SINCO (Sistema Nacional de Clasificaci\'{o}n de Ocupaciones), which I crosswalk to ISCO-08 using INEGI's official mapping. Other LAC countries either use national classifiers without published ISCO crosswalks (Chile's CIUO-CL, Brazil's CBO) or publish at coarser detail. Second, sampling representativeness. All six surveys are nationally representative at quarterly frequency, with one exception: Peru's quarterly module (ENPE) covers only Lima Metropolitana, because the 2023 redesign of ENAHO removed the quarterly national employment module and migrated quarterly employment publication to the geographically restricted ENPE. The Peruvian estimate $\hat\tau^{\text{Peru}}$ should therefore be read as Lima-Metropolitana-specific, not nationally representative. I flag this caveat again when discussing the country-specific results.

The time window is a deliberate design choice. Pre-2020 periods are excluded even where data exist. COVID-19 differentially affected high-exposure cognitive occupations (remote-work migration, sectoral reallocation) relative to low-exposure manual and service occupations, so combining pre-COVID and post-COVID years would compare labor markets in two structurally different equilibria. The 2021Q1 start date ensures that the pre-treatment and post-treatment periods are both drawn from the post-COVID steady state. The trade-off is a short pre-period --- seven quarters in the quarterly countries, two years in the annual countries. I mitigate it by elevating the Honest-DiD bounds of \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honest} from robustness to core identification, by replacing temporal placebo dates (2019, 2021) with cross-sectional placebos on $\beta = 0$ occupations, and by exploiting a longer post-period (twelve quarters or three years) to offset the shorter pre-period in aggregate sample size.

The unit of observation in country $c$ is the individual worker $i$ at time $t$, with the 4-digit ISCO-08 occupation $o(i)$ recorded in the survey. For aggregate event-study and dose-response specifications, microdata are aggregated to the (occupation, time) cell within each country.

\subsection{Treatment variable}
\label{subsec:method_treatment}

The treatment is continuous occupational exposure to LLMs, measured by the Eloundou complementary-exposure score $\beta_o$ \citep{Eloundou2023_gpt_exposure}. The score is the share of an occupation's tasks where at least 50 percent of the time required can be reduced through software \emph{built on top of LLMs} (LLM-augmented applications), as distinct from $\alpha_o$ (direct LLM substitution) and $\zeta_o = \alpha_o + \beta_o$ (the upper bound); see Table 1 of \citet{Eloundou2023_gpt_exposure}. Throughout, $E_{o} \equiv \beta_o$ is the operative exposure variable in the estimating equation.

The choice of $\beta$ rather than $\zeta$ is theoretically motivated. The complementary channel ($\beta$) captures the diffusion of LLM-augmented software --- third-party applications, productivity tools, customer-relationship platforms, accounting systems, customer-service chatbots, and code assistants built on LLM APIs --- through which workers experience LLM capability without necessarily typing into a chat interface. In Latin America, direct ChatGPT use lags OECD levels but multinational firms deploy LLM-augmented enterprise software, so $\beta$ is the correct treatment intensity for this setting. It is also more conservative than $\zeta$. The score is mapped from O*NET to ISCO-08 (the internal pipeline routes through SOC-2010 as a bridge, but the user-facing crosswalk is O*NET-to-ISCO-08). After applying the crosswalk, 98.2 percent of 4-digit ISCO codes with positive employment in any of the six countries match at least one O*NET occupation; unmatched codes are assigned at the 3-digit level. The crosswalk builds on and extends the Chile-Mexico-Peru pilot validated in \citet{Azuara2024_idb_ai}.

As alternative exposure measures, \cref{sec:robustness} reports estimates using $\alpha_o$ (direct LLM substitution), $\zeta_o = \alpha_o + \beta_o$ (the upper bound), the Webb patent-based measure \citep{Webb2020_ai_exposure}, and the Felten AIOE index \citep{Felten2023_ai_exposure}. \citet{Handa2025_economic_tasks} provide complementary evidence on realized usage by mapping Claude conversations to O*NET tasks.

The post-treatment indicator is $\text{Post}_t = \mathbb{1}[t \geq 2023\text{Q1}]$. The 2022Q4 quarter is dropped from the primary specification because Q4 averages are two-thirds pre-launch, which would bias $\tau$ toward zero if the quarter were coded as treated. A robustness specification includes 2022Q4 as treated to provide a conservative lower bound; this matches the convention in \citet{HumlumVestergaard2025_llm} and \citet{Hartley2024_labor_effects}.

\subsection{Primary specification: country-by-country continuous DiD}
\label{subsec:method_primary}

The primary estimator is the continuous-treatment DiD of \citet{CallawayCGBS2024_continuous}, applied separately to each of the six countries. The treatment date is identical across countries: $t^* = 2022$Q4 in every case, because the November 2022 ChatGPT launch was a single global event. The target parameter is the country-specific Average Causal Response on the Treated (ACRT) along the dose dimension, averaged over the country's exposure distribution. After aggregating microdata to the (occupation, time) cell, the specification estimated within country $c$ is
\begin{equation}
Y_{ot}^{c} \;=\; \tau^{c} \cdot \bigl(E_{o} \cdot \text{Post}_t\bigr) \;+\; \alpha_{o}^{c} \;+\; \alpha_{t}^{c} \;+\; \varepsilon_{ot}^{c},
\label{eq:did_main}
\end{equation}
where $Y_{ot}^c$ is one of three primary outcomes (employment rate, average weekly hours, or average log hourly wage) in country $c$'s (occupation, time) cell, $E_{o} \equiv \beta_o \in [0,1]$ is the Eloundou complementary-exposure score for the 4-digit ISCO occupation, $\text{Post}_t = \mathbb{1}[t \geq 2023\text{Q1}]$, $\alpha_{o}^c$ is an occupation fixed effect (within country), $\alpha_{t}^c$ is a time fixed effect (within country), and $\varepsilon_{ot}^c$ is an idiosyncratic error. Worker-level controls (age, age-squared, gender, education category, urban indicator, household-head status) are absorbed at the cell-construction stage by aggregating outcomes within demographic cells before the regression.

The coefficient $\tau^c$ is the country-$c$ ACRT parameter. The occupation fixed effect $\alpha_{o}^c$ absorbs the level of $E_o$ (which is time-invariant within an occupation) and the country-specific baseline occupational wage structure. The time fixed effect $\alpha_{t}^c$ absorbs country-specific macro shocks, including post-COVID recovery and inflation. What remains is within-occupation variation in how the outcome responds to the post-launch indicator, weighted by exposure intensity. \Cref{tab:fe_identification} clarifies what each fixed-effects stack identifies within a single country.

The headline result of the paper is the side-by-side comparison of $\{\hat\tau^c\}_{c=1}^6$ across the six countries, reported in Table~1 of \cref{sec:results} with one column per country, each row showing the country-specific point estimate, standard error, and Honest-DiD breakdown bound. Cross-country variation in $\hat\tau^c$ is the empirical object of interest.

I also estimate the dose-response curve non-parametrically within each country by partitioning exposure into quintiles and estimating ATT for each quintile relative to the lowest:
\begin{equation}
Y_{ot}^c \;=\; \sum_{q=2}^{5} \tau_q^c \cdot \mathbb{1}[E_{o} \in Q_q^c] \cdot \text{Post}_t \;+\; \alpha_{o}^c \;+\; \alpha_{t}^c \;+\; \varepsilon_{ot}^c,
\label{eq:dose_response}
\end{equation}
where $Q_q^c$ are the country-$c$ employment-weighted exposure quintiles. Monotonicity of $\{\tau_2^c, \ldots, \tau_5^c\}$ is a testable implication of a clean dose-response relationship within country $c$.

Cells are weighted by survey-expanded employment for population representativeness, with unweighted estimates reported as robustness per \citet{SolonHW2015_weight}. Standard errors are clustered at the 4-digit ISCO occupation level within country, the unit at which $E_o$ varies \citep{AbadieAIW2023_cluster}. For specifications with fewer than fifty clusters (heterogeneity-cell analyses within smaller countries), I use the wild cluster bootstrap via \texttt{fwildclusterboot}.

\subsection{Secondary estimators}
\label{subsec:method_secondary}

Two secondary estimators triangulate the primary result within each country. The first is the binary-cutoff DiD of \citet{CallawayS2021_did}:
\begin{equation}
Y_{ot}^c \;=\; \tau^{c,\text{bin}} \cdot (D_o \cdot \text{Post}_t) \;+\; \alpha_{o}^c \;+\; \alpha_{t}^c \;+\; \varepsilon_{ot}^c,\qquad D_o = \mathbb{1}[E_o > \text{median}^c(E)].
\label{eq:did_binary}
\end{equation}
This specification is reported because it is the identification design used by \citet{Hartley2024_labor_effects}, \citet{Hui2024_online_labor}, and \citet{Liu2025_generate_future}, allowing a direct magnitude comparison. The cutoff is the country-specific employment-weighted median ($p50$). I use the country-specific median rather than the $>50\%$ theoretical threshold because Eloundou's $\beta$ has a long left tail; only 5--10 percent of ISCO-08 occupations exceed 0.5, so a $>50\%$ cutoff produces extremely unbalanced groups and large standard errors. The median split, used by \citet{Liu2025_generate_future}, yields balanced cells while preserving the binary interpretation. The continuous specification (the paper's primary) is unaffected by this choice. Robustness reports alternative cutoffs at the $>50\%$ theoretical threshold, at the 25th and 75th percentiles, and at the country-pooled median.

The second secondary estimator is the Sun-Abraham interaction-weighted event study \citep{SunAbraham2021_eventstudy}, estimated within each country:
\begin{equation}
Y_{ot}^c \;=\; \sum_{\substack{k=-K \\ k \neq -1}}^{K} \theta_k^c \cdot \bigl(E_{o} \cdot \mathbb{1}[t - t^\ast = k]\bigr) \;+\; \alpha_{o}^c \;+\; \alpha_{t}^c \;+\; \varepsilon_{ot}^c,
\label{eq:event_study}
\end{equation}
where $t^\ast = 2023\text{Q1}$ is the treatment date and $k = -1$ is the reference period. The quarterly event study covers $k \in [-12, +8]$ for the five calendar-quarter countries; the annual event study covers $k \in [-2, +3]$ for Peru and Uruguay. The expected pattern is flat $\theta_k^c$ for $k < -1$ (parallel pre-trends) and a step change at $k = 0$ that either persists or grows.

A country-specific shift-share specification follows \citet{GoldsmithPinkham2020_bartik} and \citet{BorusyakHJ2022_shiftshare}. For each local labor market $\ell$ in country $c$, I construct a predetermined occupation-share exposure index $Z_{\ell c} = \sum_o \text{emp}_{o, \ell c, 2021} \cdot E_o$ using 2021 employment shares (the first post-COVID year, predetermined relative to ChatGPT by two years) and estimate
\begin{equation}
Y_{\ell t}^c \;=\; \gamma^c \cdot (Z_{\ell c} \cdot \text{Post}_t) \;+\; \alpha_{\ell}^c \;+\; \alpha_{t}^c \;+\; \varepsilon_{\ell t}^c.
\label{eq:bartik}
\end{equation}
The shift in this design is country-specific (the country's own ChatGPT-era adoption shock); the shares are also country-specific (each country's 2021 local employment composition). Rotemberg weights are reported in an appendix table with a narrative discussion of the top-weighted occupations within each country. Robustness re-estimates dropping the top-weighted occupation and using 2021--2022 average shares. Inference uses the Adão-Kolesár-Morales standard errors via \texttt{ShiftShareSE}.

\subsection{Identifying assumptions}
\label{subsec:method_assumptions}

The country-by-country specification identifies $\tau^c$ under four assumptions, evaluated separately within each country.

\emph{Parallel trends.} Within country $c$, for any exposure level $e$, the counterfactual outcome trajectory for treated occupations would have evolved in parallel with the trajectory for zero-exposure occupations absent the LLM shock. Formally, $\mathbb{E}[Y_{ot}^c(0) - Y_{o,t^\ast - 1}^c(0) \mid E_{o} = e] = \mathbb{E}[Y_{ot}^c(0) - Y_{o,t^\ast - 1}^c(0) \mid E_{o} = 0]$ for all $e$ and all $t \geq t^\ast$. This assumption is defended within each country with the Sun-Abraham pre-trend event study, a joint test that $\theta_k^c = 0$ for $k < -1$, cross-sectional placebos on zero-exposure occupations, and partial-identification bounds following \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honest}. Given the short pre-period, the Honest-DiD bounds are reported as core identification within each country; headline claims for country $c$ require a country-specific breakdown value $\bar M^c \geq 2$.

\emph{No anticipation.} Workers in country $c$ did not adjust labor-supply or employment outcomes in anticipation of ChatGPT prior to its launch. The launch was exogenous to LAC labor markets: pre-release access was restricted to OpenAI employees and selected external testers, none of whom operated in LAC labor markets at the scale needed to move aggregate outcomes. A weak residual anticipation channel via the GPT-3.5 API (released March 2022) is bounded by the event-study coefficients $\theta_{-2}^c$ and $\theta_{-1}^c$ within each country.

\emph{Exposure exogeneity.} The $\beta$ exposure score is constructed from O*NET task content (US, 2022--2023) using GPT-4 and a human rubric. Neither the task content nor the rating procedure is a function of country-$c$ 2023 labor-market outcomes. Task content within ISCO codes is stable across countries; this was validated in \citet{Azuara2024_idb_ai} for Chile, Mexico, and Peru. Robustness using $\alpha$, $\zeta$, the Webb patent-based measure, and the Felten AIOE confirms that within-country results do not depend on the specific Eloundou complementary score.

\emph{SUTVA.} The stable unit treatment value assumption rules out cross-occupation spillovers within a country. Intra-firm spillovers bias toward zero: if high-exposure colleagues raise low-exposure colleagues' productivity, the estimated displacement effect understates the true dose-response. Industry-by-time fixed effects within country bound the spillover magnitude in robustness; the narrative bound of \citet{BergBZ2018_ai_inequality} suggests that with below-20-percent adoption, general-equilibrium spillovers across occupations are modest. SUTVA is the most at-risk assumption and is acknowledged as such for every country estimate.

Temporal placebo tests at 2019 or 2021 are infeasible given the post-COVID sample window. I substitute cross-sectional placebos that restrict estimation to $\beta = 0$ occupations within each country (the true-zero control group) and placebo outcomes that should not respond to LLM exposure (weekly hours of agricultural manual laborers).

\subsection{Within-country heterogeneity: baseline formality as the central margin}
\label{subsec:method_heterogeneity}

Baseline formality status $F_{i,2021}$ is the pre-registered central within-country heterogeneity dimension for every country in which formal-informal segmentation is non-trivial. I use \emph{baseline} rather than contemporaneous formality because contemporaneous $F_{ict}$ is itself an outcome of LLM exposure: a worker may transition formal-to-informal because of treatment, and conditioning on a post-treatment outcome is a bad control. Baseline $F_{i,2021}$ is the worker's formality status as of the last pre-2023 observation and is econometrically predetermined with respect to the ChatGPT shock.

The imputation protocol depends on the survey structure. For Mexico's ENOE (a rotating panel at the household level, not a worker panel for cross-country pooling), $F_{i,2021}$ is the worker's last observed pre-2023Q1 formality status; workers who entered after 2023Q1 are dropped from the formality-interaction analysis. For the five repeated cross-sections (Colombia GEIH, Peru ENAHO/ENPE, Costa Rica ECE, Ecuador ENEMDU, Uruguay ECH), $F_{i,2021}$ is cell-imputed as the 2021 share of formal workers in the worker's (4-digit ISCO occupation, 5-year age bin, gender, education tier, urban) cell within country $c$. Sensitivity analysis uses 3-digit ISCO cells.

The within-country interaction specification (estimated separately for each country) is
\begin{equation}
Y_{ot}^c \;=\; \tau_F^c \cdot (E_{o} \cdot \text{Post}_t \cdot F_{o,2021}^c) \;+\; \tau_I^c \cdot (E_{o} \cdot \text{Post}_t \cdot (1 - F_{o,2021}^c)) \;+\; \alpha_{o}^c \;+\; \alpha_{t}^c \;+\; \varepsilon_{ot}^c,
\label{eq:formality_interaction}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_F^c$ and $\tau_I^c$ are the ACRT coefficients for baseline-formal and baseline-informal cells within country $c$, and $F_{o,2021}^c$ is the imputed baseline formality share for occupation $o$ in country $c$. The four narratives in \cref{subsec:method_narratives} are evaluated within each country in terms of the signs and magnitudes of $(\tau^c, \tau_F^c, \tau_I^c)$.

Other pre-registered within-country heterogeneity dimensions are gender, education (tertiary vs.\ less than tertiary), age (15--29, 30--49, 50+), 1-digit ISIC sector, skill level (1-digit ISCO), and urban/rural. Causal forests \citep{AtheyWager2018_forests} provide an exploratory data-driven complement, run within each country.

\subsection{Robustness layer}
\label{subsec:method_robustness}

The robustness layer is run within every country. I report Honest-DiD bounds from \citet{RambachanRoth2023_honest} with the breakdown value $\bar M^c$ for every country headline. Cross-sectional placebos restrict the country sample to $\beta = 0$ occupations. Placebo outcome tests use variables that should not respond to LLM exposure (hours worked by agricultural manual laborers). Alternative exposure measures ($\alpha$, $\zeta$, Webb, Felten) are substituted for the $\beta$ score within each country. A 3-digit ISCO fallback addresses small-cell concerns. Alternative binary cutoffs are reported at the 25th, 33rd, 66th, and 75th percentiles within each country. The wild cluster bootstrap \citep{AbadieAIW2023_cluster} is used for specifications with fewer than fifty clusters via \texttt{fwildclusterboot}. The Bartik specification is reported with country-specific Rotemberg weights and re-estimated dropping the top-weighted occupation. Oster bounds \citep{Oster2019_unobservables} are reported with $\delta = 1$ and $R^2_{\max} = 1.3 R^2$ via \texttt{sensemakr} for each country headline; if the identified set includes zero, the country headline is flagged as not robust and requires Honest-DiD $\bar M^c \geq 2$ to be retained. Multiple-testing corrections follow Romano-Wolf within dimensions and Bonferroni across dimensions, targeting family-wise error rate ten percent within each country.

\subsection{Four pre-registered narratives, evaluated per country}
\label{subsec:method_narratives}

The identification strategy is designed to speak to four empirical patterns, pre-committed before the analysis is run, and \emph{evaluated within every one of the six countries}. Each country reports which of the four patterns its $(\hat\tau^c, \hat\tau_F^c, \hat\tau_I^c)$ supports. The headline empirical contribution is therefore six independent tests of the same set of narratives, with cross-country variation in which narrative obtains as the central finding.

\textbf{Narrative A} -- \emph{Informality amplifies}. In country $c$: $|\hat\tau^c| > 2\%$ and $\tau_I^c < \tau_F^c < 0$. Informal workers absorb the shock more than formal workers in country $c$.

\textbf{Narrative B} -- \emph{Null replication}. In country $c$: $|\hat\tau^c|$ is small and statistically indistinguishable from zero in both channels. Country $c$ is a LAC null replication of \citet{HumlumVestergaard2025_llm}.

\textbf{Narrative C} -- \emph{Null in formal, large in informal}. In country $c$: $\tau_F^c \approx 0$ and $\tau_I^c$ meaningfully negative. Country $c$ identifies a channel that Danish and US designs cannot detect because their informal sectors are negligible.

\textbf{Narrative D} -- \emph{Stratified gains}. In country $c$: $\tau^c > 0$ on log wages for high-exposure occupations, concentrated in tertiary-educated urban formal workers ($\tau_F^{c,\text{tertiary, urban}} > 0$); employment effects near zero. LLMs act as a skill-biased productivity enhancer for the protected segment in country $c$.

Pre-committing the narratives is itself a methodological contribution. Which narrative the paper reports for country $c$ in the final draft is determined by the realized empirical pattern in country $c$, not by specification search. The pre-analysis plan is deposited on OSF before the country-by-country estimates on 2023+ data are run. The six independent applications of the same set of narratives sharpen the test. If one narrative obtains in all six countries, that is evidence on the universality of the mechanism. If different countries support different narratives, that is evidence on the role of country-specific institutions.
